Compliance guide

How often, to what standard, with what proof.

The three questions every kitchen operator eventually asks about exhaust compliance — answered plainly, from AS 1851-2012 as it's applied in practice.

01  Service frequency

Your cooking volume
sets the clock.

AS 1851-2012 ties maintenance to the system, and in practice the service interval is set by how hard the kitchen works the system. The profiles below are the industry's working baseline — your site's actual schedule is confirmed at assessment.

Usage profileWhat that looks likeIndicative full-system serviceFilters
Heavy Solid-fuel cooking, woks, char grills, heavy frying, 24-hour or high-volume operations Quarterly Weekly–fortnightly exchange
Moderate Full-service à la carte, standard hotel and club kitchens, steady daily covers Six-monthly Fortnightly–monthly exchange
Light Low-volume kitchens, reheating and light café cooking, limited trading hours Annually Monthly exchange

Filters always run on a shorter cycle than the system they protect — a clean filter set does not make a compliant system, and a compliant system still chokes behind neglected filters. Frequencies above are indicative baselines consistent with AS 1851-2012 as applied to usage and with the AIRAH best-practice guidance for commercial kitchen exhaust management; the schedule for your site is set — and documented — at assessment.

02  The proof

A schedule without records
is a promise, not compliance.

Whoever services your system, hold them to the same evidence bar. A defensible record shows:

01A certificate that names AS 1851-2012 — not “cleaned to industry standard”.
02Measured grease-deposit readings, before and after.
03Photographs of each component in found and finished condition.
04Limitations declared with reasons — and the next service date set.

03  New South Wales

In NSW, the record feeds your Annual Fire Safety Statement.

Each year, NSW building owners lodge an Annual Fire Safety Statement (AFSS) confirming that every essential fire safety measure on the building's fire safety schedule has been assessed by an accredited practitioner. Where the kitchen exhaust system is listed, that assessment leans on one thing: the maintenance evidence.

Summit's records are built for that hand-over — standard-named certificates, measured readings and photo sets your practitioner can rely on, held current and produced in minutes, not reconstructed under deadline.

04  Common questions

Asked by every operator.
Answered plainly.

How often should a commercial kitchen exhaust system be cleaned?

It depends on how hard the kitchen works the system. As a working baseline under AS 1851-2012: heavy-use kitchens (solid fuel, woks, 24-hour or heavy frying) sit around quarterly, moderate full-service kitchens around six-monthly, and light-use kitchens around annually — with filters on a much shorter cycle throughout. The actual schedule for your site is set at assessment and documented on the record.

What must a compliance certificate actually contain?

The standard, named — AS 1851-2012 — plus the system and scope serviced, the result, any limitations declared, the next due date, and a cross-reference to the full record and photo set. If a certificate doesn't name the standard, assume it won't survive scrutiny.

Will a generic “cleaning certificate” satisfy my insurer?

Often not. After a kitchen fire, maintenance records are among the first documents an insurer requests, and they look for a dated evidence chain — standard-named certificates, measured readings, photographs. A certificate that asserts a clean without evidencing it invites dispute at exactly the moment you can least afford one.

What's the difference between filter cleaning and a full system clean?

Filters catch grease at the canopy and need attention weekly to monthly depending on load — often via a managed exchange program. The full system service covers canopy, plenum, ductwork, riser and fan on the longer usage-based interval. Clean filters alone don't make a compliant system — and a certified duct clean doesn't excuse choked filters.

Do you clean the whole duct run or just what's reachable?

The scope is the whole system. Where a section genuinely can't be reached, the limitation goes on the record with the reason and a proposed remedy — typically an access panel. What we won't do is leave a section silently unserviced; an undeclared gap in the record becomes your problem at audit.

What is the AFSS, and how does exhaust cleaning relate?

The Annual Fire Safety Statement is the NSW building owner's yearly confirmation that each essential fire safety measure has been assessed by an accredited practitioner. Where the kitchen exhaust system is on the building's fire safety schedule, the practitioner's assessment relies on maintenance evidence — the records and certificates described on the evidence pack.

Do we need to shut the kitchen?

No — servicing is planned around the operation, typically overnight or after service, and scheduled ahead as part of the program. The kitchen opens as normal.

How does this work across a multi-site group?

One partner, one schedule, one consolidated record. Every site's obligations sit on a single managed program, records held current per site, and the whole portfolio position readable at a glance — see compliance & asset maintenance programs.
